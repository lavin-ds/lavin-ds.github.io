How I Think About Story Pointing

Story pointing is the process of assigning an abstract number to a particular task in the Agile software development world. Software teams come together each sprint in a process called planning and estimation. This is when the team members go through tasks and stories together and assign points to them. It helps managers and engineers in giving a rough estimate of the work involved or the time that will be taken by a particular task. Emphasis on rough estimate.

Story pointing, for me, is not about how an individual points stories, but how people do it as a team. When a new member in a team points their first story, the numbers may be way off, and it might be hard to find alignment across the team for the varying numbers. Overtime, the team comes to an unspoken understanding of how much work each abstract story point is worth, and you will start to see the story points starting to align. This will be particularly true for tasks that the team has performed multiple times.

A greenfield piece of work may have the same problem as a new team or team member. Each person on the team will have varying estimates of how to do it. This is where the power of this estimation strategy lies. Some people in the team may have thought of hidden gotchas in the piece of work, which could be why they are pointing higher or lower. The gap in the estimation will then lead to a discussion about why each person believes their estimate to be right, which will lead to peeling the layers of work back and revealing the actual effort involved.

So I think of story pointing as a ratio system. If my team thinks modifying a data structure is worth a single story point, then creating a new API endpoint in an existing service may be worth five points. A brand-new service, might need thirteen story points. A different team might set the same tasks to be five, twenty and forty story points respectively

Summarizing the main points below -

  • Story points will mean different things to different teams
  • A single story point for your team might not equal the same amount of effort involved in a single story point for another team
  • The amount of effort involved in a single story point should remain stable for “your” team each sprint, and it should remain stable from one story to another unless you start doing something very new or onboard a new team member that doesn’t have the context yet.
  • The number you assign doesn’t matter - what matters is the ratio. The story points should help you demonstrate relative effort between each story every sprint
  • If during a teams’ estimation session, a particular piece of work leads to story points which are very far from each other, the reasoning should be that there is new work that some members of the team have not done before.

Personally I prefer story point numbers to be as small as possible. It means we have thought about the topic well enough to have broken it into its finest possible pieces. Which in turn means working on it will be a breeze as the work is already broken down in a granular fashion.
