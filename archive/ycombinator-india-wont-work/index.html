Why the Y Combinator Model Won't Work for India!

Firstly, for all you folks who are wondering what the hell the title is, let’s just start with a little info about what the Y Combinator model is.

In 2005, Y Combinator a venture capitalist developed a new model of startup funding. Twice a year they invest a small amount of money (average $18k) in numerous startups (currently 60). The startups move to Silicon Valley for 3 months, during which they work intensively with them to get the company into the best possible shape and refine their pitch to investors. Each cycle culminates in Demo Day, when the startups present to a large audience of investors. Basically a YC model works on two factors â€“ First the availability of angel investors, and secondly amazing startups. India unfortunately has a shortage of both.

Our present market scenario has either a large no of small profitable companies never growing big, and one or two market kings doing the same thing they’ve done ever since they began. The market for a virtual business is very tiny here.

What startups need here is money. A hell lot of it. Plain hard work, and lots of effort is going to get you nowhere. If you want to make a mark and get somewhere then you definitely need to have a good capital base. This further creates trouble. Investors and incubators who are trying to keep companies alive are doing just that. They back a company and that’s all they do. They don’t stick around to nurture their growth. All they expect is the start-ups would put in all their man-hours and create a cyber revolution. But that’s just wasted effort.

Even now when we hear amazing things from people about there being a great number of start-ups in our own version of Silicon Valley, that is all it is. We hear them. There are not that many success stories. These startups start and then they just stop.

This is what is further causing our so-called angel investors from distancing themselves away from startups. They don’t see the merit of pouring their wealth into companies that don’t look promising.

This is a very sorry state of affairs for our country because the loser here is talent. And if more and more investors follow the trend, all the opportunity for a wonderful growth is just going to go down the drain.
